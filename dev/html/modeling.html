MODELING

The goal of our project is to produce Mycosporine-like Amino Acids(shinorine and porphyra-334) through four reactions catalyzed by 4 different enzymes. (REF)

The initial goal of our model was to predict the yield of the reaction. For this we have constructed a mathematical model using Matlab Simbiology tools(file attached). We have derived ODEs for all the reactions(presented below).

The first reaction is monosubstrate and the following three are bisubstrate.

  1. sedoheptulose-7-phosphate⟶desmethyl-4-deoxygadusol + phosphate + H2O
  2. desmethyl-4-deoxygadusol + S-adenosyl-L-methionine⟶4-deoxygadusol + S-adenosyl-L-homocysteine
  3. 4-deoxygadusol + glycine + ATP⟶4mycosporine-glycine + ADP + phosphate + H
  4. mycosporine-glycine + ATP + serine/threonine ⟶4shinorine/porphyra-334 + ADP + phosphate+H2O
  1. demethyl-4-deoxygadusol synthase
  2. demethyl-4-deoxygadusol methyltransferase (O-MT family)
  3. 4-deoxygadusol glycyltransferase
  4. D-ala-D-ala ligase (ATP-grasp)

ODEs:

  1. d[D4D]/dt=Vm[S7D]/(Km+[S7P])
  2. d[4D]/dt=Vm[SAM][D4D]/KsKm2+Km1[SAM]+Km2[D4D]+[SAM][D4D]
  3. d[M-Gly]/dt=Vm[4D][ATP]/KsKm1+Km1[4D]+Km2[ATP]+[4D][ATP]
  4. d[MAA]/dt=Vm[M-Gly][ATP]/KVsKVm1+KVm1[M-Gly]+KVm2[ATP]+[M-Gly][ATP]

However, we have encountered a sudden problem: kinetic constants for the majority of used enzymes were not yet determined. We successfully gathered constants only for the mysA enzyme from the Nostoc punctiforme.

We have tried to find more information about the homological enzymes in similar organisms to have at least the rough approximation, but were unable to find any. In our research we used BRENDA, KEGG, UniProt. We have also attempted to reach the researchers working in this field but they were unable to provide required informationlink to IHP page

At first, we expected that the lack of information would still allow to construct dependency graphs, but the amount of unknown constants were too big and the analytical solution turned out too complex for MATLAB to interpret the relation in the form of the graph.

It allows to output the graphs of the concentration to the time span for the products of all 4 reactions.

Despite lacking the values for the constants, with the help of this script we still were able to visualise the influence of the concentration changes in precursor and intermediates on the yield.

We have came to the conclusion, that our model can be used in the future when our system will start working and we will be able to approximate the constants by our own measurements. However, at the point our project is now it is impossible due to the time limitations. Nevertheless, we are planning to perform possible examinations in the future.

After failing to make a fully developed kinetical model and therefore making appropriate conclusions about the yield of the final products we have started to look for other options.

One of the specialists that we consulted (Ranko Gacesa) has noted that the rate constants measurements in vivo are rarely done, and in vitro measurements can be not very reliable. For that reason, we might have to run our own experiments to determine firstly the level of gene expression and secondly the enzyme activity.The choice of the promoters will depend on both of these factors since the enzymes catalyze the series of reactions the product of one being the substrate of another.

In our initial plan we intended to introduce the genes as a whole cassette using one of the three methods (Vegas, Gibson or CPEC) and only perform HPLC measurements for the final products (shinorine and porphyra-334). However R.Gacesa has recommended us to introduce genes one by one and control their work in a stepwise manner.

For this we have decided to use a SBY2 yeast strain free from selective markers and 4 centromeric yeast plasmids (Ura,Leu,His and Trp selection) and do 4 simultaneous transformations with one, two, three and four genes and check every step of the pathway.

When we will be able to acquire some initial information about the flux in the pathway, we will make some conclusions about the strength of the promoters that we have to use for each gene of our system to smooth out and regulate the flux through our pathway we have started to look for other options.

We were inspired by thehttps://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-018-03293-x#MOESM1paper and used some of their promoters in our work. We have chosen 5 promoters and rated them by the relative strength from 1 to 5. The total number of combinations is equal to 5^4 since we have 4 genes. However, it is really hard to test all of them and we need to make some predictions and assumption in order to narrow down the options. That is why we need the initial test. In our first strain we planned to use the strongest promoter (TDH3) with each gene. We assumed, that it will make gene expression level almost equal and will allow us to track down enzyme activities.

However, due to the time limits we were not able to run HPLC measurements on time. Therefore, we had to simplify the model using only theoretical assumptions.

Our idea was that with every step there will be natural deprivation in the concentrations of the products. Consequently, it is logical to assume that it is better to use stronger promoters for the first step gradually decreasing the strength of the promoter with every step.

During the research dedicated to the rate constants, we have also discovered that the concentration of the precursor: sedoheptulose 7-phosphate (S7P) is low in our model organism (S. cerevisiae) and this will negatively affect the yield of the final products.

Therefore, we have decided to redirect our modeling toward the optimization of the S7P concentration. S7P is an intermediate metabolite in the pentose phosphate pathway. It consists out of two phases: oxidative and non-oxidative. The first one is used to produce NADPH that is later used in the reductive biosynthesis, and C5 sugars like ribulose 5-phosphate. During the second (where S7P is produced) C5 sugars are converted into C3 and C6 ones.https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK22416/

In addition, S7P can also be produced outside the ppp by the enzyme SHB17.(figure1)

Our first plan was to change the growth media to the xylulose. This way we are forcing yeast to follow the anaerobic pathway of xylose fermentation. S. Cerevisiae are unable to ferment xylose, but they can utilize its isomer - xylulose.(all the different xylose fermentation pathways lead to its formation, Dxylulose later participates in the non-oxidative phase of the ppp).We are increasing the S7P concentration by the addition of its precursor into the media.

However, this approach has a significant drawback: the yield change is small compared to the price difference between the usual glucose media and xylulose one. Moreover, the media described in the article we used consists both out of xylose and xylose and requires specific preparation. In addition, the cell mass production and media consumption rate are lower. It is also proven that the growth rate on the xylulose depends on the efficiency of the non-oxidative phase of ppp. That means, that the slower the consumption of the S7P(which we are trying to minimize), the slower the growth is.(The non-oxidative pentose phosphate pathway controls the fermentation rate of xylulose but not of xylose in Saccharomyces cerevisiae TMB3001)https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16348083

This disadvantages makes this method useless for our project, since we are seeking our system to be cost-effective and easy to use in the industry.

Next, we started to analyse the PPP and the S. Cerevisiae genes participating in it. In addition, we also looked at the genes that lead to the production of the S7P.https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4470864/

The following information about the genes is taken from the open KEGG database:

  1. Tal1(Transaldolase, enzyme in the non-oxidative pentose phosphate pathway; converts sedoheptulose 7-phosphate and glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate to erythrose 4-phosphate and fructose 6-phosphate)
  2. NQM1(paralog of tal1)
  3. Tkl1 and Tkl2(Transketolase; catalyzes conversion of xylulose-5-phosphate and ribose-5-phosphate to sedoheptulose-7-phosphate and glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate in the pentose phosphate pathway; needed for synthesis of aromatic amino acids)
  4. Zwf1(Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD); catalyzes the first step of the pentose phosphate pathway)
  5. Shb17(Sedoheptulose bisphosphatase involved in riboneogenesis; dephosphorylates sedoheptulose 1,7-bisphosphate)
  6. pgi1(Phosphoglucose isomerase, catalyzes the interconversion of glucose-6-phosphate and fructose-6-phosphate)
  7. Pho13 (phosphatase, induces tal1, «PHO13 deletion-induced transcriptional activation prevents sedoheptulose accumulation during xylose metabolism in engineered Saccharomycescerevisiae»)

Genes that induce the consumption of S7P: Tal1, NQM1, TKL1, TKL2

Genes that induce the production of S7P: SHB17

Our first plan was to change the expression of these genes, put weaker promoter to the Tal1, NQM1, TKL1 and TKL2 and stronger one to SHB17.We can also put stronger promoter to the first gene from the MAA biosynthesis pathway to redirect the flux of S7P.

We were also thinking about knocking down some genes to achieve even better outcome.The tal1 null mutant is viable; the null mutant of paralog nqm1 is viable; the tal1 nqm1 double mutant is inviable or displays a growth defect. The tkl1 null mutant is viable; the null mutant of paralog tkl2 is viable; the tkl1 tkl2 double mutant is viable but auxotrophic for aromatic amino acids.(Information about the viability of the null mutants is taken from the open source yeastgenome.org)

It is also proven that the deletion of tal1 gene in combination with pgi1 significantly increases the yield of S7P. Change of the media to glucose also positively affects the yield of the S7P.https://febs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/j.1432-1033.1990.tb15440.x

In addition, we have found the research, where the simultaneous deletion of tal1 and nqm1 increases the flux through shb17. In our opinion, in this manipulation with the combination with a strong promoter to shb17 can give high production of S7P.https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3163394/

Double null mutant(tal1,tkl1) is also viable, however there is no information about the concentration of S7P in this strain.https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8299150

Here is presented a logical pathway, that illustrate, how the expression of certain genes affect the concentration of S7P.

Figure 1 Logical pathway

Switches represent the presence and absence of the genes, while the red light represents the active production of the S7P.(figure2)

Between TKL1 and TKL2 we have put XOR gate, since only the absence of one of the genes(not both) will increase the S7P concentration and simultaneously not reduce the growth rate significantly.

Pho13 and Pgi1 increase the influence of the deletion of Tal1, therefore, they are connected with the AND gate.

However, XOR gate between these genes and zwf1 symbolize less critical fluctuations in the triple mutant.

The following OR gate represents that both knockouts change S7P concentrations but not interrupt the influence of each other.

The last gate is AND that represents the accumulative effect of the deletions.

In our opinion, it would be beneficial to switch off TKL1, TAL1, NQM1, PHO13, PGI1
